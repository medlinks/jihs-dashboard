## Section B: Unwritten Rules—What Reviewers Want

### Framing & Editorial Appetite
1. **"Real-world deployment + retrospective validation"** wins over "novel algorithm benchmark." Editors favor papers showing 2+ years of operational use with documented case studies (e.g., NYC SaTScan since 2014; Netherlands surveillance 2015–2023).
2. **Geographic/national scope** matters. Multi-country or subnational systems (47 prefectures in Japan, 8 countries in forecasting study) signal scalability and public health relevance. Single-center studies are harder to publish.
3. **Burden of disease or actionability** must be explicit. Why these 108 diseases? Why 14 years? Papers succeed when they tie alert volume/specificity to concrete downstream use (e.g., investigation, containment).

### What Triggers Desk Rejection
Per JMIR instructions and observed patterns:
- **No evaluation experiment.** Demo-only or "here's our dashboard" without any sensitivity/PPV/AUC/case-study comparison.
- **Weak public-health significance.** Algorithm achieves 95% AUC on toy data but unclear how alerts change practice.
- **No baseline or prior method comparison.** How does your hybrid detection vs. classic historic limits, Farrington, or EARS?
- **Missing evaluation metrics.** For outbreak detection: sensitivity, positive predictive value (PPV), timeliness, and specificity are expected. For dashboards: user evaluation or expert appraisal (e.g., PANDEM-2's participatory review).
- **Insufficient Methods detail.** Too vague on alert algorithm parameters (your 5-year median window, z≥2/3 thresholds *must* be specified with justification).

### Methods Section Expectations
Typical structure for "Development Study":
1. **Study Design & Setting** – national/multi-prefectural surveillance; time period; diseases/population covered.
2. **Data Source & Preprocessing** – IDWR/JIHS data extraction; handling of missing values; geographic/temporal aggregation.
3. **Alert Algorithm** – Layer 1 & Layer 2 logic with pseudocode or flowchart; parameters & hyperparameters; reference period (5-year baseline) clearly documented.
4. **Evaluation Framework** – train/test split or retrospective validation period; metrics (sensitivity, PPV, time-to-alert, false-positive rate).
5. **Dashboard Implementation** – technology stack (web, API, visualization library); user interface design rationale.
6. **LLM Commentary & Triage** – LLM model name, prompt engineering, validation (human review %, inter-rater agreement).
7. **Statistical Analysis** – no fancy ML needed; describe hypothesis tests or descriptive comparison.

### Results & Figures
- **Mandatory:** Time series plots showing alert threshold (historical ±2-week bands) overlaid on actual case counts; document 5–10 real outbreak/anomaly case studies.
- **Expected:** Sensitivity/specificity ROC or confusion matrices; false-positive rate by disease class (rare vs. endemic).
- **Recommended:** Heatmap of alert frequency by disease×prefecture; cumulative detection curves; LLM commentary examples (N=3–5 with human assessment).
- **Supplementary:** Detailed parameter sensitivity analysis (what if baseline window = 3 years, or z≥2.5?); list of missed signals and reasons.

### Discussion Must Address
1. **Comparison with prior work** – Explicitly compare your two-layer approach against published Farrington, historic limits, CUSUM implementations. What did they miss that you catch?
2. **Generalizability limits** – Does IDWR data quality/completeness vary by prefecture or disease class? How would your system perform on new diseases or pandemic-induced underreporting (cite van Deursen et al. 2025)?
3. **Implementation burden** – Staff time for triage? Cost? Transition plan from existing surveillance workflows?
4. **False positives** – Be honest: how many alerts per week/month? How are they triaged (LLM + human review)? What % are actionable?
5. **Public health outcomes** – Did your alerts enable faster outbreak response? Cite case studies or expert feedback (e.g., participatory validation as in PANDEM-2).
6. **LLM limitations** – Hallucinations? Bias in commentary generation? How do you ensure quality (human review %, inter-rater κ)?
7. **Ethics & transparency** – Data privacy, retention, who has access? How are false alerts communicated to prefectures?

### Reporting Checklists
- JMIR requires **Submission Preparation Checklist** (standard for all development studies).
- For **validation/evaluation studies**, consider attaching STROBE (Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology) or similar checklist.
- No mandatory CONSORT, but papers with clinical trial or RCT components need it.

### Reference Count & Structure
- **Typical:** 40–60 references for an original development/surveillance paper.
- **Expected citations:** Include 2–3 methodological anchors (Farrington, historic limits, EARS papers), 5–10 recent surveillance system implementations, 3–5 dashboard/UX papers, 2–3 LLM in healthcare.
- **Word count:** 5,000–7,000 words (excluding abstract, figures, references).

5. **LLM-Generated Weekly Commentary**
   - Model (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini—specify); prompt engineering (attach exemplars); output format (structured JSON + narrative); validation method (N=?? weeks manually reviewed; inter-rater agreement κ on quality/accuracy).
   
6. **JIHS News/Alert Triage System**
   - LLM-assisted classification (e.g., relevant vs. noise); comparison with baseline keyword matching; human curator workflow.
   
7. **Dashboard Architecture & Visualization**
   - Technology stack; frontend (web browser, mobile?); backend (Python, Node, R Shiny?); real-time refresh frequency.
   - UI design rationale; accessibility considerations; user feedback (if available).
   
8. **Statistical Analysis**
   - Primarily descriptive (no fancy ML needed). Time series plots, heatmaps, confusion matrices. Note: no hypothesis tests needed for surveillance system papers—focus on sensitivity/specificity documentation.

### Evaluation Experiments to Run
1. **Case Study Documentation** – Select 8–10 documented outbreaks (measles, influenza, dengue in specific prefectures) and show: (a) alert threshold timeline, (b) when your system triggered, (c) time-to-detection vs. manual notification, (d) outcome (outbreak confirmed/contained).
2. **Sensitivity Benchmark** – Compare your Layer 1+2 detection against standalone historic limits (5-year median + z-score) and Farrington algorithm on the same 69,120 weekly records. Show ROC or sensitivity/PPV curves.
3. **False Positive Analysis** – Quantify alert burden: how many Layer 1 + Layer 2 alerts per week/month? What % are deemed actionable by epidemiologists? (Aim for <2–3 false alerts/week to stay acceptable.)
4. **LLM Commentary Accuracy** – Have 2–3 senior epidemiologists rate N=50 LLM-generated weekly summaries on accuracy (0–10 scale), informativeness, and adherence to guidelines. Report inter-rater κ and mean scores.
5. **System Stability** – Show weekly alert counts across full 14-year period; document any parameter tuning interventions; assess for "alert fatigue" drift.
